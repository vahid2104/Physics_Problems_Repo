Circular Motion

This interactive page explains the motion of a body moving on a circle of radius R with angular velocity ω.

Acceleration vector a

1. Mathematical Model

In uniform circular motion, the angular position changes linearly with time:

θ(t) = ωt

If the center of the circle is at the origin, then the position vector is:

r(t) = (R cos(ωt), R sin(ωt))

The velocity vector is the derivative of the position:

v(t) = dr/dt = (-Rω sin(ωt), Rω cos(ωt))

The acceleration vector is the derivative of the velocity:

a(t) = dv/dt = (-Rω² cos(ωt), -Rω² sin(ωt))

2. Magnitudes

The magnitudes of the vectors stay:

|r(t)| = R |v(t)| = Rω |a(t)| = Rω²

This means:

  • The object always stays at the same distance from the center.
  • The speed is constant in uniform circular motion.
  • The acceleration has constant magnitude, but its direction changes continuously.

Another important formula is:

|a| = v² / R

3. Why is the acceleration centripetal?

From the formula of acceleration:

a(t) = (-Rω² cos(ωt), -Rω² sin(ωt))

We can factor out -ω²:

a(t) = -ω² (R cos(ωt), R sin(ωt))

But

(R cos(ωt), R sin(ωt)) = r(t)

Therefore:

a(t) = -ω² r(t)

This shows that acceleration always points in the opposite direction of the position vector. Since the position vector points outward from the center to the body, the acceleration points inward toward the center.

That is why the acceleration is called centripetal acceleration.

4. Interpretation of the vectors

  • r points from the center of the circle to the particle.
  • v is tangent to the circle, so it shows the direction of motion.
  • a points toward the center of the circle.

In circular motion:

v ⟂ r a ∥ -r

So:

  • Velocity is perpendicular to the radius.
  • Acceleration is opposite to the radius direction.
