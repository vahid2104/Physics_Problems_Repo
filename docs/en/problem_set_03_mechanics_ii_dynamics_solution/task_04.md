# Problem 4 – Conservation of energy

A body falls from a height (h) without air resistance.

We want to determine:

* the total energy (E(h)=T(h)+U(h)),
* the velocity as a function of height (v(h)),
* the comparison with the result from Newton’s second law,
* the height at which the kinetic energy is 75% of the total energy.

---

## 1. Total energy

The total mechanical energy is the sum of:

* kinetic energy (T),
* potential energy (U).

So,

$$
E(h)=T(h)+U(h)
$$

The kinetic energy is

$$
T(h)=\frac{1}{2}mv(h)^2
$$

The gravitational potential energy is

$$
U(h)=mgh
$$

Therefore,

$$
E(h)=\frac{1}{2}mv(h)^2+mgh
$$

Since there is **no air resistance**, the mechanical energy is conserved.

So the total energy stays constant during the motion.

If the body is released from rest at initial height (h_0), then initially

$$
v=0
$$

so the initial total energy is

$$
E_0=mgh_0
$$

Thus at any height (h),

$$
\frac{1}{2}mv(h)^2+mgh=mgh_0
$$

---

## 2. Velocity as a function of height

From conservation of energy,

$$
\frac{1}{2}mv(h)^2+mgh=mgh_0
$$

Subtract (mgh) from both sides:

$$
\frac{1}{2}mv(h)^2=mg(h_0-h)
$$

Now divide by (m):

$$
\frac{1}{2}v(h)^2=g(h_0-h)
$$

Multiply by 2:

$$
v(h)^2=2g(h_0-h)
$$

Take the square root:

$$
v(h)=\sqrt{2g(h_0-h)}
$$

Therefore, the velocity as a function of height is

$$
v(h)=\sqrt{2g(h_0-h)}
$$

This shows that the lower the body falls, the greater its speed becomes.

---

## 3. Compare with Newton’s second law

Now let us compare this with the result from Newton’s second law.

The only force acting is gravity:

$$
F=mg
$$

From Newton’s second law,

$$
ma=mg
$$

So the acceleration is

$$
a=g
$$

For motion starting from rest and falling a distance

$$
s=h_0-h
$$

we use the kinematics formula

$$
v^2=v_0^2+2as
$$

Since the body starts from rest,

$$
v_0=0
$$

and since (a=g),

$$
v^2=2g(h_0-h)
$$

So,

$$
v(h)=\sqrt{2g(h_0-h)}
$$

This is exactly the same result as the one obtained from conservation of energy.

Therefore, the energy method and Newton’s second law give the same velocity formula.

---

## 4. Height where kinetic energy is 75% of the total energy

We want the kinetic energy to be 75% of the total energy.

So,

$$
T=0.75,E
$$

The total energy is constant and equal to

$$
E=mgh_0
$$

Thus,

$$
T=0.75,mgh_0
$$

But also,

$$
T=mg(h_0-h)
$$

because the loss of potential energy becomes kinetic energy.

So we set

$$
mg(h_0-h)=0.75,mgh_0
$$

Cancel (mg) from both sides:

$$
h_0-h=0.75h_0
$$

Solve for (h):

$$
h=h_0-0.75h_0
$$

$$
h=0.25h_0
$$

Therefore, the kinetic energy is 75% of the total energy when the body is at height

$$
h=\frac{h_0}{4}
$$

So the body has fallen three quarters of the initial height.

---

## Final answers for Problem 4

The total energy is

$$
E(h)=\frac{1}{2}mv(h)^2+mgh
$$

and since energy is conserved,

$$
E(h)=mgh_0
$$

The velocity as a function of height is

$$
v(h)=\sqrt{2g(h_0-h)}
$$

Using Newton’s second law also gives

$$
v(h)=\sqrt{2g(h_0-h)}
$$

so both methods agree.

The kinetic energy is 75% of the total energy when the body is at height

$$
h=\frac{h_0}{4}
$$
